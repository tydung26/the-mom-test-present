# Brainstorm: The Mom Test Storytelling Redesign

**Date:** 2026-04-16
**Status:** Approved

## Problem Statement

Current 24-section presentation covers The Mom Test comprehensively but lacks narrative arc. Feels like a list of tips, not a transformative story. User wants to restructure around 1-2 core messages with discovery story format.

## Discovery Summary

### Audience
- 2/3 software engineers / AI practitioners
- 1/3 marketing, HR, sales
- Mixed technical/non-technical

### Desired Transformation
**Delusion → Clarity**
- From: "My idea is great, I just need to explain it better"
- To: "I've been fooling myself"

### Narrative Structure
**Discovery story** — Journey of uncovering why smart people build wrong things

## Two Core Messages

### Message 1: Truth is Uncomfortable
> "Real customer insight feels bad. If it feels good, it's probably fake."

Book foundations:
- "Opinions are worthless"
- "Anything involving the future is an over-optimistic lie"
- "People will lie to you if they think it's what you want to hear"
- "Bad customer conversations convince you that you're on the right path"
- False positives cause over-investment of cash, time, and team

### Message 2: Seek Commitment
> "Words are cheap. Real signal = they give up something of value."

Book foundations:
- "You want facts and commitments, not compliments"
- "A compliment costs them nothing, so it's worth nothing"
- "Commitment — showing they're serious by giving up time, reputation, or money"
- "The more they're giving up, the more seriously you can take their kind words"
- "It's not a real lead until you've given them a concrete chance to reject you"

## Section Details

### Section 1: Hook
**Quote:** "We talk to customers... and still end up building stuff nobody buys."

Sets up the universal problem. Everyone in audience (engineers, sales, marketing) has experienced this.

### Section 2: The Fail (Cookbook/iPad Conversation)
From book — son pitching iPad cookbook app to mom:
- "Would you ever buy an app which was like a cookbook for your iPad?"
- Mom: "That sounds amazing... Will it have pictures?"
- Son completely misinterprets and takes it as validation

**Key insight:** Mom's internal monologue reveals she's lying to protect his feelings.

### Section 3: Message 1 — Truth is Uncomfortable
Core rules from book:
- "Opinions are worthless"
- "Anything involving the future is an over-optimistic lie"
- "People will lie to you if they think it's what you want to hear"

**Punch:** "If the conversation felt good, the data is probably fake."

### Section 4: Why We're Fooled — 3 Types of Bad Data
1. **Compliments** — "They said they loved it!" (worthless)
2. **Fluff** — Generics, hypotheticals, the future
3. **Ideas** — Feature requests without understanding motivation

### Section 5: The Fix — The Mom Test
Three rules:
1. Talk about their life, not your idea
2. Ask about specifics in the past, not generics about the future
3. Talk less and listen more

**Key:** "If you just avoid mentioning your idea, you automatically start asking better questions"

### Section 6: The Pass (Same Conversation, Done Right)
Same mom, same cookbook topic, totally different approach:
- "What's the last thing you did on it?"
- "Where did you find out about the apps you use?"
- "What's the last cookbook you bought for yourself?"

**Gold dust answers:**
- Old people don't need generic recipes
- Gift market may be strong
- Younger cooks may be better segment
- Experienced chefs buy niche/specialty cookbooks

### Section 7: Message 2 — Seek Commitment
> "You want facts and commitments, not compliments."

**Core insight:** Commitment = they're giving up something of value. Compliments cost nothing.

### Section 8: The Currencies of Commitment
**Time commitments:**
- Clear next meeting with known goals
- Using a trial for non-trivial period

**Reputation commitments:**
- Intro to peers or boss
- Public testimonial

**Financial commitments:**
- Letter of intent
- Pre-order / deposit

**Rule:** "The more they're giving up, the more seriously you can take their kind words."

### Section 9: Product Risk vs Market Risk
**Product risk:** Can I build it? Can I grow it? Will they keep using it?
**Market risk:** Do they want it? Will they pay? Are there enough of them?

**Examples:**
- Video games = pure product risk (don't need to validate "do people like fun?")
- Farm fertility monitor = product risk (farmer says "if you can build it, I'll buy it")
- Nightclub foot traffic app = product risk (building the audience is the hard part)

**Key:** If heavy product risk, can't prove business through conversations alone.

### Section 10: Customer Slicing
**Problem:** "Advertisers" as customer segment = drowning in mixed signals.

**Solution:** Keep slicing until you have a **who-where pair**.
- Who: Specific demographic with specific motivation
- Where: Concrete place to find them

**Example:** "Students" → Non-native speaking PhD students with upcoming conference talks → Find through department advisors

**Rule:** "If you aren't finding consistent problems and goals, you don't yet have a specific enough customer segment."

### Section 11: Good vs Bad Meeting
| Statement | Verdict | Why |
|-----------|---------|-----|
| "That's so cool. I love it!" | BAD | Pure compliment, zero data |
| "Let me know when it launches" | BAD | Compliment + stall tactic |
| "I would definitely buy that" | DANGER | False positive that sunk a $10M startup |
| "What are the next steps?" | GOOD | Advancement signal |
| "Can I buy the prototype?" | GOLD | Real commitment |

**Fix:** Push for commitment at end of every meeting.

### Section 12: One Thing Monday
**Takeaway:** "If this existed, would you pay now?"

Forces the shift from hypothetical to concrete commitment. If they say yes, take their money. If they hesitate, you learned the truth.

**Closing:** Q&A
